Pipsqueak: Simple Game Mechanics

Small and simple game mechanics for fast and fun tabletop roleplaying games. By Chris Palmer. Version 2022-09-27.

Introduction

Pipsqueak is a suite of rules and mechanics for tabletop roleplaying games that emphasizes ease of learning, speed of play, and adaptability to a wide range of roleplaying settings. It aims to help you play challenging, cinematic games with a minimum of time spent researching finer points and lawyering.

A key design principle of Pipsqueak is continuity over discreteness: that is, characters grow gradually instead of in sudden leaps of ability, as in systems with levels and feats. Small actions cause small effects, and actions of gradually increasing power have gradually increasing effects. There are no hard boundaries between character classes, as in other games: any character can grow in any direction their player chooses. Nothing stops a warrior from picking up a few tricks of the rogue’s trade, just as any farm-hand or scavenger can learn to quiet their mind and feel the Force flow.

Pipsqueak also aims to use a minimum of tools and gear. To play you need only some 6-sided dice, pencil and paper, and this book. (Miniature figurines and paper maps can also be a big help.) When running as a live web page, this book contains simple calculators to ease the flow of arithmetic during play. And, of course, emulated dice, should you need them.

Attributes And Skills

What a character can do in the game, and how well they perform at it, is determined by their skill scores. In turn, each skill is governed by 1 of the 6 fundamental attributes. Every character has some base score for each of the 6 attributes, which is the basis for their ability to use skills governed by those attributes.

When players have their characters attempt to achieve feats in the game, there is always an element of chance. Attributes and skills are rated with numeric scores that indicate the character’s ability to achieve their goals in different skill areas, by indicating how many dice the player is to roll and sum up. (Higher sums are better.) The Core Mechanics explains these scores and how to use them in gameplay.

Attributes That Define A Character

There are 6 attributes that define a character’s mental and physical aptitudes for various skills.

Agility
represents a character’s physical flexibility, coordination, speed, and balance.
Constitution
represents not only a character’s raw muscle power in various parts of their body, but also resistance to pain, endurance, and overall health.
Knowledge
represents a character’s aptitude for learning the raw facts of things in the world: languages, art, history, cultures, nature, and more.
Perception
represents a character’s ability to perceive the world around them, their attentiveness to what they see, and their ability to understand and predict the behavior of other beings.
Mechanical
represents a character’s ability to understand the mechanical interactions and relationships between physical things such as machines, animals and people, construction and buildings, vehicles, and so on.
Technical
represents a character’s ability to understand the logical interactions and relationships between notional things such as mathematics, magical entities and powers, software, circuits, and so on.

Each attribute governs various skills. For example, Acrobatics is governed by Agility, and Piloting is governed by Mechanical. A character with no special training in a given skill uses the dice score of the governing attribute as their skill score for any rolls using that skill.

Bug, a very Technical being (4D) with Security skill score of 6D and Repulsorlift Craft Repair of 4D, has easily broken into and hotwired a landspeeder and gleefully hopped into the driver’s seat. However, Bug does not have very high Mechanical aptitude — just 2D — and has no additional skill in Piloting (Repulsorlift). “Do you know how to drive this thing?” Serai asked, skeptically.

“Learn by doing, I say!” cackled Bug, and turned on the ignition. Bug will roll 2D for Piloting (Repulsorlift).

“I have a bad feeling about this,” sighed Serai.

Characters that represent normal, everyday people in the game typically have dice scores of 2D on all their attributes, and 3D to 6D in whatever skills they specialize in. Heroes and villains are special, and typically have an average of 3D on their attributes. Their skills may have very high scores.

Each skill description below includes a skill time, which is the amount of in-game time required to use the skill. Some skills have several times; the gamemaster will tell players which one applies based on the nature of the skill use and the game context.

Some skills have “reaction” as their skill time, which means that they happen instantaneously, and in reaction to some other skill use. Typically, these reaction skills are defensive skills like Dodge and Melee Parry.

The Core Mechanics

Much of a roleplaying game involves players declaring that they want their characters to perform various actions, like piloting a starfighter, duelling with pirates, or doing an emergency field repair on a crashed airspeeder. Different characters have different aptitudes and abilities, and hence different likelihoods of success at different tasks.

For each action they can perform, each character has a dice score or skill score, expressed as a number of dice the player should roll. For example, 3D means to roll 3 6-sided dice and sum the total, and 8D means to roll 8 6-sided dice and sum the total. (Pipsqueak uses 6-sided dice exclusively.) Higher totals indicate a greater likelihood of success.

Each roll is compared to a difficulty rating, with higher ratings indicating more difficult actions. The action succeeds if the roll total is equal to or greater than the difficulty rating.

Dice And Pips

The dice score may also have an additional modifier, such as −1, −2, +1, or +2. (But not lower than −2 or higher than +2, for reasons discussed below.) For example, 4D−1 means to roll 4 dice, sum the total, and then subtract one. 5D+2 means to roll 5 dice, sum the total, and then add 2.

The dots on dice are called pips, so we sometimes pronounce “6D+2” as “roll 6 dice and add 2 pips”. Just as we abbreviate “3 dice” with “3D”, “1p” means “1 pip”.

Die And Pip Arithmetic

Sometimes we add or subtract dice scores together when calculating a character’s effective ability to perform an action. For example, wearing Stormtrooper armor increases a character’s Constitution (only for the purpose of resisting damage) by +1D, while at the same time reducing all of the character’s Agility skill rolls by −1D. (See Attributes And Skills and Weight And Encumbrance.)

When the dice scores are simple, without any pip modifiers, the arithmetic is simple. For example, most Stormtroopers have a Firearms (Light) skill code of 4D, and they subtract 1D from that when wearing armor to get an effective score of 4D − 1D = 3D. Similarly, their Constitution is usually 2D, and their effective ability to resist the Rebels’ blaster fire is thus 2D + 1D = 3D.

It’s still pretty easy when 1 of the scores has a pip modifier. For example, a swashbuckling pirate with 5D+2 in Melee Attack would have an effective score of 5D+2 − 1D = 4D+2 if they wore Stormtrooper armor.

But what if more than 1 of the scores involves pips? For example, Gimli the dwarf has Constitution 3D+2, and he wields a great axe in combat. The great axe does Constitution + 1D+2 damage when Gimli hits his foe. To calculate Gimli’s effective damage, we add the dice and then we add the pips: 3D+2 + 1D+2 = 4D+4. However, as we saw above, no pip modifier is ever greater than +2 or less than −2 —  a third pip is equivalent to an additional die. To resolve this, subtract 3 pips and ‘carry the 1’ over to the number before the D: 4D+4 → 5D+1.

Math nerds might recognize this as base-3 arithmetic. Why 3? Because we’re using 6-sided dice: on average, we should expect to roll about 3.5 on a 6-sided die. (For more detail on dice probabilities, see e.g. The Wizard Of Odds.) So once you get that 3rd pip, you’ve come pretty close, on average, to receiving the benefit (or penalty, if the modifier is negative) of a whole new die.

A dice score can never go below 1D in any case. Every character can always roll at least 1D to perform an action — and since that die is wild, they may even succeed against all odds!

Difficulty Rolls

Some actions require the player to roll equal to or greater than a given number to succeed in their goal. The more difficult the task, the higher the number. The gamemaster will decide how difficult the task is and what its difficulty score will be. For some guidelines and examples, see the Basic Skill Difficulty Chart.

Opposed Rolls

Sometimes a character actively opposes the actions of another character. In these cases, a single set difficulty rating does not suffice. As a simple example, consider 2 characters arm-wrestling. There is no single difficulty; it’s a contest of strength against strength. In a situation like this, each character would roll their Constitution score, and the high roller would win. In case of a tie, the characters would roll again.

In other cases, there may be a set difficulty, but a character may perform some action to try to make the difficulty higher for their adversary.

The bounty hunter Jane has a Dodge skill score of 5D. She’s at medium range from the Stormtroopers blasting at her, so the Stormtroopers have difficulty 15. With 5D, there’s a good probability that Jane can roll higher than 15. She rolls and gets a 20, so now the Stormtroopers need to roll 20. If Jane had rolled lower, she would actually decrease the difficulty for the Stormtroopers —  unfortunately, it’s possible to stumble into fire!

The Wild Die

Sometimes a character will have a dice score for an action that is too low to ever roll high enough to meet a given difficulty rating. For example, the gamemaster might declare that to jump across a chasm requires a roll of 20 with the Athletics (Land) skill, but your character only has a score of 3D —  it is impossible to roll more than 18 with 3D.

However, to account for the mysteries of the universe, incredible luck, and plucky heroism, it is always just barely possible, however unlikely, to succeed. We model this with the wild die: identify 1 of your dice as the wild die, and if you roll a 6 on it, you get to roll it again and add that number to your total. If that roll is also a 6, roll again and add it again! And so on.

Kate is playing a character named Serai, a noblewoman and Rebel spy. For Serai to leap across the chasm, Kate needs to roll 20, but Serai’s Athletics (Land) score is only 3D. But the Stormtroopers are right behind her, so it’s certain death either way! Serai runs and desperately leaps. Kate rolls 4, 2, and then 6 on her wild die, for a total of 12. Rolling the wild die again, Kate rolls a 6 again! Rolling the wild die yet again, Kate rolls a 3. Her total is 4 + 2 + 6 + 6 + 3 = 21. Serai just barely lands on the other side, her foot almost slipping off the edge of the ground. She takes a deep breath and then keeps running!

To reliably identify your wild die, consider using a dedicated die that looks different from your other dice.

The wild die applies only to skill rolls, not other rolls (such as damage rolls).

When Things Go South...

When you roll a 1 on all your dice, that represents a critical failure: not only does your character fail at their task, some effect counter to their goal happens. That might be just when your character’s weapon runs out of ammunition, their sonic screwdriver breaks, they open their pouch of spell components and find it empty, or they suffer a moment of total confusion at the sheer complexity when trying to slice into the computer network at Scarif.

Critical failures happen even if the total roll is equal to or greater than the task’s difficulty. It is always possible, however unlikely, that Darth Vader will trip on a rock and drop his lightsaber.

Preparing Rolls

Characters can spend 1 action round to prepare their next action: concentrating on a problem, drawing a bead on a target, or otherwise preparing to do better than usual. If they manage to prepare for the entire action round without being disrupted in any way and without performing any other actions or reactions, the player adds 1D to the skill roll in the next action round for the action the character prepared.

It is not possible to get more than 1D of advantage through preparation, no matter how many action rounds the character prepares.

What counts as a disruption is up to the gamemaster’s discretion, but certainly includes the character being successfully attacked and the character taking any reactive action skill rolls (such as Dodge). In noisy or hectic circumstances, the gamemaster might require the character to make a Perception roll to maintain concentration. (This would not count as a reactive action for the purposes of disruption, of course.)

Jessica, a brash pilot with a Piloting (Repulsorlift) score of 7D, is going to try to lose the Imperial Police by flying her swoop bike at full throttle through the narrow alleys of Coruscant. One wrong move, and splat! The gamemaster sets the difficulty of the roll at 25. Jessica circles around the University Arcology and lets her bike just coast, hoping against hope that the police miss her if they fire. Their blaster bolts whiz past, but the Force is with her today! She zeroes in on an alley entrance, takes a deep breath, and slams down the pedal. She rolls 8D.

Called Shots

A character who is particularly skilled (or lucky) can make a called shot: they can specify what particular part of their target they want to hit. To attempt this, the player must announce the intent ahead of time. To succeed, they must roll equal to or greater than twice the difficulty rating for the action.

A called shot that misses does not ‘degrade’ to a regular hit or skill success; it is a miss or failure.

A Stormtrooper in the distance has foolishly taken off his helmet, and Jane the bounty hunter would like to shoot him in the head — negating the usual Stormtrooper armor advantage of 1D. Jane’s Firearms (Light) skill score is 6D, and she has also spent the previous action round preparing a shot against this Stormtrooper. She thus has an effective skill score of 7D for this action. At medium range, the base difficulty is 15. Jane must roll 15 × 2 = 30 or better for the called shot to succeed!

Called shots are not limited to combat — they need not be shots, per se. There is a wide variety of skill uses for which called shots are useful, including Gambling (“I will draw the ace of spades from the top of the deck!”), Languages or Streetwise (“I will use obscure slang specific to this sub-culture to convince them I’m legit”), Piloting, and more.

Of course, a successful called shot is also by definition a smashing success.

Smashing Success

If a player rolls equal to or greater than twice the difficulty rating for any skill roll (not just a called shot), they achieve smashing success: they not only achieve their goal, but an additional goal as well. For example, a smashing success at Astrogation might result in a route that takes half the usual time.

However, because the player does not take the risk of failure that comes with calling a shot, they do not get to specify the additional degree or type of success. That is up to the gamemaster.

In combat, the effect of a smashing success is often to increase the damage code for the hit. For example, a smashing success with a blaster pistol (damage code 4D) might do 5D of effective damage to its target.

Collaborative Rolls

Sometimes, a group of characters combine their efforts to achieve a task. For example, perhaps several characters work together to lift a heavy steel beam. To collaborate like this, you only roll once, but you combine the skill scores of the characters. Use the skill score of the character with the highest-rated skill as a base, and then add the number of dice of each of the other characters’ skill scores as pips. Then, reduce the base score and the added pips to a dice score as usual.

The characters need to lift a very heavy steel beam out of the way, and the gamemaster has set the difficulty at 25 for a skill roll of Athletics (Land). Jane has the highest skill score, 4D+2. Bug has 2D+2, Serai has 2D+1, and Pel has 3D. Bug’s effort thus adds 2 pips, Serai’s adds 2, and Pel’s adds 3, for a total of 7p. 4D+2 + 7 = 4D+9, which reduces to 7D, so that’s what they roll. Their odds are decent!

However, collaboration requires effective leadership. One character must lead the others in the task, by rolling Command. (This character is not necessarily the one with the highest skill in the task.) The difficulty of the Command roll depends on how willing the characters are to collaborate in the first place. For details, see the Command skill.

It is also possible to collaborate this way with combat skills. Each successful collaborative roll counts as a single success. In combat terms that means a single hit and a single damage roll.

The 4 Stormtroopers decide to concentrate their fire on Serai, who has just stolen a copy of the Imperial personnel database and is running away as fast as she can. Each Stormtrooper’s effective Firearms (Light) skill is 4D − 1D armor disadvantage = 3D. That’s 3D as a base score for the first trooper, then an additional 3p for each of the remaining 3: 3D+9. 3D+9 reduces to 6D, so that’s what they roll. If they succeed, it counts as a single hit —  but the damage roll is calculated the same way. All the Stormtroopers carry blaster rifles (5D damage), so the damage is 5D + 5p for each of the other three. That’s 5D + 15 = 10D. Serai may want to try to Dodge!

It is also possible for collaborative rolls to achieve smashing success and (with sufficient training, discipline, and leadership) to be called shots.

If you use the Wild Die complication, 1 of the dice is a wild die as usual.

Hero Points

Heroes, and major villains, typically have 1 or more Hero Points which they can spend to double all their base attribute and skill scores (i.e., their attribute and skill scores before adding any other advantages or subtracting any disadvantages) for 1 action round.

Spending a Hero Point spends it permanently. However, if the character spends it in furtherance of the story, the gamemaster is likely to award the character a new Hero Point at the end of the gaming session. If the character spends the point selfishly, just to save their own bacon or otherwise solely for their own gain, they might not get a new point.

A character can only spend 1 Hero Point per action round.

With or without using a Hero Point, if a character acts particularly selflessly (for example to save other characters’ lives), the gamemaster may award that character new Hero Points at the end of the gaming session. All Hero Point awards are at the gamemaster’s discretion.

Villain Points

Villains, and heroes, may have 1 or more Villain Points. Villain Points can confer evil power; for example, it may be necessary to have some Villain Points to use certain evil magic —  or using evil magic might ‘earn’ the character a Villain Point. The specifics of this depend on the particular game setting. (See Magic for more.)

Unlike Hero Points, players don’t spend Villain Points. Instead, Villain Points represent the state of the character’s soul —  and their future as a player-character.

Every time a character gets a Villain Point, their player must roll 1 die. If the roll is less than the character’s Villain Point total, the character becomes a villain. (Note that it is therefore impossible for a character with only 1 Villain Point to become a villain.) Player-characters cannot be villains, so the player loses the ability to play this character. Players should therefore be very careful about doing evil, and gamemasters should be very careful about assigning Villain Points.

A hero can atone for their villainous deeds, and reduce their Villain Point score, by acting solely in the service of others for 1 or more gaming sessions (as the gamemaster sees fit).

Action

Action (movement, combat, hijinks) takes place in rounds of about 5 seconds of game-world time. Each round is broken up into 1 or more action segments, during which the results of characters’ actions are resolved.

The Order Of Actions

First, each player decides what they want their character to do in the current round. The gamemaster decides what the NPCs will do.

Then, going around the table, each player and the gamemaster announce what their characters will do. At this point, take note of which characters’ actions may affect each other. (See more details below.)

Action Segments

As each player and the gamemaster announce what their characters will do, they may announce that their character will (try to) perform more than 1 action. Each distinct action happens in its own action segment, during which each character attempts 1 action. The order in which actions in each segment occur is determined by initiative.

After each character’s action has been resolved, the action round then progresses to the next segment, during which players who had declared additional actions for their characters resolve those actions. The round contains as many segments as are necessary to resolve all announced character actions. Depending on what the players try to have their characters do, a given action round might contain 1 segment, or 5, or more.

Initiative

If 2 or more characters declare actions in the same segment that would interfere, such as if the hero and villain both intend to attack each other, roll both actions as normal. The action with the higher roll occurs first. If the action succeeds and would cause the opposing action to fail, the opposing action does not occur.

An Example Combat Round

The PCs Bug (a slicer), Sass (a student of the Force), and Jane (a bounty hunter) are inside the Star Destroyer Unforgiving, having stolen a top-secret Imperial intelligence personnel database, and are trying to escape back out into the hangar bay. A squad of 4 Stormtroopers and their captain are hot on the trail. The captain has remotely locked the blast door to the hangar bay.

Gamemaster: You run up to the blast door, but it doesn’t open —  the light on the lock panel is blinking red. The captain yells, “Eliminate the bounty hunter first, and then we’ll deal with the rest of them!” What do you do?

Movement

The Athletics skills (Land, Air, and Water) represent a character’s skill and speed at movement. For the purposes of initiative —  flying out of range before an adversary can shoot, running a race, and so on —  roll the appropriate Athletics skill.

The Athletics skills also represent how far a character can move in 1 action round. If we assume that the average Earth human can sustain a walking speed of about 4 miles (6,436 meters) per hour, and if we model the average Earth human to have a Constitution score of 2D and no additional skill in Athletics (Land), we find that they can walk about 9 meters per 5-second round: 6,436 meters ÷ 60 minutes ÷ 12 5-second rounds per minute = 8.9 meters per round.

From there, we can extrapolate that 1D of Athletics (Land) skill represents a walking speed of about 4.5 m/round. So a character with 3D in Athletics (Land) could walk 13.5 m/round, a character with 4D could walk 18 m/round, and so on.

For normal running, double the walking speed. For desperate running or racing, triple it.

In difficult terrain, including uphill, a character’s effective speed is reduced by a factor of 2 or more (at the gamemaster’s discretion).

If a character runs for longer than a few minutes, or walks for more than a few hours, they will need to succeed at successively more difficult Endurance skill rolls for suffer Fatigue.

Reach

In melee combat, a weapon’s reach is as critical a concern as range is in ranged combat. When you have a dagger, it’s more difficult to parry an attacker’s long sword —  the sword can reach farther than the dagger, and can keep the dagger at bay.

When rolling Melee Parry, calculate the defender’s reach (dis)advantage as follows:

Melee Parry (dis)advantage = defender’s reach − attacker’s reach

Captain Alio’s vibrosword has reach 3p, and she is bearing down on an Imperial Stormtrooper who is trying to parry with a vibrodagger (reach 1p). This results in a significant disadvantage for the poor Stormtrooper: 1p − 3p = −2p. Similarly, when parrying the Stormtrooper’s stabs, Alio’s advantage is 3p − 1p = 2p.

For weapons with 1.3m (4p) reach or more, the defender’s effective reach becomes 0 if the attacker can get inside that distance.

Leonidas has valiantly fought off the first wave of Immortals with his long pike (a pole-arm with reach 2m, 6p). However, one of them managed to parry the attack despite the great disadvantage —  the Immortals earned their name! — and has closed in on Leonidas’ space. To avoid being at a disadvantage against the Immortal’s scimitar (a short sword with reach 2p), Leonidas grimaces, drops the pike, and draws his xiphos (also a short sword).

Weapon Properties

Different weapons have different properties that affect how (and whether) characters can use them.

2-handed
2-handed weapons require 2 hands to wield effectively. For species with no additional arms, this precludes wielding any additional weapons.

Gimli strolled out onto the field with a mug of beer in one hand, and his great axe in the other. He stared at the line of orcs, who jeered as he chugged. Suddenly, they charged at him! With a sigh, he dropped the mug and gripped the axe in both hands, ready to swing. Such a shame to have to waste good brew like that.

Finesse
Finesse weapons are easy enough to manipulate that a skilled warrior can choose to use either Constitution or Agility as the basis for the weapon’s Damage score. To take advantage of finesse, the character’s Melee Attack score (or Throw score, when throwing throwable finesse weapons) must be greater than the damage rating would be if based on the character’s Agility.

Dread Pirate Roberts has Constitution 3D, Agility 3D+2, and Melee Attack 4D. Normally, his rapier has a damage rating of 3D (Constitution) + 1D = 4D. To wield his rapier with finesse —  thus dealing 3D+2 (Agility) + 1D = 4D+2 damage — he’ll need to achieve greater than 4D+2 in Melee Attack skill: that is, 5D or better.

Heavy
Heavy weapons require high strength to wield effectively, and cannot be used together with other melee weapons (as in a dual-wield attack). When wielding these weapons, attackers suffer a disadvantage on their Melee Attack rolls equal to their Constitution dice score (not counting any remaining pips) − 5.

Conan The Barbarian has Constitution 4D, so is at a 4 − 5 = −1p disadvantage when he swings his great sword. Conan’s friend Bob The Barbarian has Constitution 3D+1. If he borrows Conan’s sword, he swings at a 3 − 5 = −2p disadvantage.

Light
Light weapons pose no additional disadvantage when the warrior is dual-wielding.

Baerd and his rapiers were a blur as he ran, parrying and lungeing through a rank of Dragonclaws. It was as if there were 2 fearsome drow, not 1.

Throwable
Throwable weapons can be thrown. The thrower’s Constitution and the weapon’s weight determine the effective range.

Annie Chapman crouched in the shadows behind a broken down ox-cart. Slowly, quietly, she drew from her boot the dagger Elizabeth had given her. Just when the top hat turned the corner, she whipped the blade 3 meters down the alley. There was a gasp, and then the Ripper’s body slumped to the ground.

Versatile
Versatile weapons can be wielded with 1 or 2 hands. When used with 2 hands, the weapon’s damage score increases by 1p.

Seeing the Taira horseman begin his charge, Tomoe raised her long sword in both hands. Thundering hooves, ringing steel, a scream — then silence. Tomoe lowered the weapon and let out a slow breath.

For weapon details, see the Basic Melee Weapons Chart and the Basic Ranged Weapons Chart.

Thrown Weapons

Weapons that have the throwable property (see below) have an effective range determined by the thrower’s Constitution dice score (not counting any remaining pips) and the weight of the weapon itself.

range unit in meters = Constitution dice ÷ weapon kg
short range: < 1 unit
medium range: < 2 units
long range: < 3 units

Misha’s Constitution is 3D+1, and she wants to throw a dagger (0.6 kg). 3 ÷ 0.6 = 5 meters. For her, short range is less than 5m, medium range is less than 10m, and long range is less than 15m. Beyond that, the dagger falls to the ground.

You can use the Thrown Range Tool to calculate thrown item ranges.

Armor

Armor is quite handy when the villains are blasting at you! Armor increases a character’s effective Constitution score only for the purposes of calculating damage by some amount of dice and/or pips. For some types of armor, the advantage may vary by the type of damage inflicted.

At the same time, armor is typically bulky and heavy, especially the more effective it is. Therefore, armor also typically decreases a character’s effective Agility score for all rolls, and the additional weight and encumbrance might result in the character needing to make Endurance checks. Characters

This chapter covers everything you need to know about creating and developing your own characters (both PCs and, for gamemasters, NPCs).

Building Characters

Although the gamemaster may provide templates or pre-created characters for players to play, you can also build a new character completely from scratch. This section describes how.

Character Type

First, decide what kind of character you want to play. A rakish gambler? A princess with a will of iron? A rogue who creates their own justice? Before doing anything else, sketch out your character’s personal history, goals and motivations, fears, and so on. Consider writing out a quote or some lines of dialogue involving your character to get a feel for how they would act.

Consider how other characters might perceive your character. Does your character see themselves as a hero while others see them as a fool, like Don Quixote? (Or vice versa?) Do people underestimate your character, like R2-D2 or the hobbits of the Shire?

Attributes

Each hero or villain character starts with 18D worth of attribute dice to distribute across the 6 attributes as the player (for PCs) or gamemaster (for NPCs) sees fit. You could divide them evenly, yielding 3D on each attribute, or put 2D on some and 4D on others. You could even go as low as 1D or as high as 5D (see Species for when this is allowed).

It is also possible to break a die up into 3 pips, and distribute the pips across attributes.

Chris is creating a martial artist named Grunff. He starts by putting 3D on each of the 6 attributes, and then makes adjustments. The defining features of a martial artist are Agility and Constitution, so Chris moves 1D from Technical to Agility, yielding 2D Technical and 4D Agility. To increase Grunff’s Constitution, Chris breaks up 1 of the 3D Grunff has on Mechanical into 3 pips. He puts 2 pips on Constitution and leaves the last on Mechanical. Grunff’s attribute scores are now: Agility 4D, Constitution 3D+2, Knowledge 3D, Perception 3D, Mechanical 2D+1, and Technical 2D.

Think carefully about your character’s attribute scores before making them final. They will affect everything your character does.

Skills

Next, the gamemaster will give you a certain number of skill points that you can spend increasing your character’s skills above the baseline attribute scores. Exactly how many skill points you get is at the gamemaster’s discretion, but a reasonable starting number would be 40. For higher-level campaigns where the characters need to start out more powerful, the gamemaster may allot more.

You spend skill points to increase your character’s skill scores by pips and dice. How many skill points a pip costs is relative to how high the skill score already is: 1 pip costs as many skill points as the skill score currently has dice.

Grunff’s Melee Attack skill starts at 4D, since his Agility is 4D. To increase it by 1 pip to 4D+1, Chris would need to spend 4 skill points. Increasing it to 4D+2 would cost another 4, and then increasing it from 4D+2 to 5 would cost another 4. At this point, pips get more expensive: it would cost 5 more skill points, not 4, to increase the skill to 5D+1.

When spending your skill points, keep an eye on how well-balanced your character’s skills are. If you spend all your points in 1 or 2 skills, you may end up with a character that can’t do much a lot of the time, or who can’t participate in much of the gameplay. (Games are rarely all combat!) The gamemaster might also set a limit on how high newly-created characters’ skills can go.

Equipment And Money

Given your character’s type and skills, consider what equipment they are likely to have (or want). How much equipment, and at what quality and quantity, also depend on the character’s resources. A prince or merchant is likely to have more money and equipment than a rogue who grew up as a street urchin, for example. A mercenary or soldier may have been issued a standard set of gear and weapons, while a priest or wizard will usually have the basic tools of their trades.

The gamemaster may allow you to simply declare a reasonable set of equipment, or may give your character a set amount of money and then you can buy what equipment you can.

Magic Abilities

Depending on the game setting, your character might have or be able to get magical abilities, psychic powers, or advanced technology that seems like magic (such as the cybernetic implants available to citizens of the Culture). The rules for these special powers are specific to the setting.

Limitations, Vices, Weaknesses

Nobody is perfect, including heroes. You may decide to give your character weaknesses or limitations, or the gamemaster may impose one (perhaps to offset a special power). For example, a wizard might have an irrational fear of a certain school of magic. A character might be too small or too large to use certain equipment, mounts, or vehicles. A character might have a physical disability, or be especially sensitive to certain substances.

Species

Characters can be of any species appropriate for the game setting. A fantasy setting may have elves, orcs, gnomes, and others, as well as (or instead of) humans. A science fiction or space opera setting can have any number and kind of aliens.

The maximum attribute dice code for normal humans is 4D. Other species may have higher limits, at the gamemaster’s discretion. For example, Wookiees are famously strong, and Wookiee heroes may have Constitution scores as high as 5D. It’s probably not a good idea, for game balance, to allow species to have attribute limits higher than 5D (but see Droids And Robots, below).

Different species may also have special abilities or limitations, again at the gamemaster’s discretion. For example, certain alien species may be especially vulnerable or resistant to certain chemicals — perhaps the Klarthon people can only breathe ammonia, for example, and find an Earth-like atmosphere poisonous.
