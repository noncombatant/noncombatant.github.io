Autodidact Software Engineering Bibliography

11 August 2013

What little I know about software engineering, I largely taught myself through books, conversations with more knowledgeable friends, and just plain getting my hands on some code. Of all the books I‚Äôve read, here are some of the ones I think helped me the most when I was coming up, and some new ones that are good.

Beginning

The C Programming Language, by Brian Kernighan and Dennis Ritchie. Even if you never end up using C, this book is a masterpiece of concise technical writing. Modern languages have excellent texts too: The Go Progamming Language by Alan Donovan and Brian Kernighan, and The Rust Programming Language, by Steve Klabnik and Carol Nichols.

The¬†Practice of Programming by Rob Pike and Brian Kernighan. Also a masterpiece of concision, and also rewards multiple readings. Covers lots of ground in an approachable and fun way.

Programming Pearls and More Programming Pearls, by Jon Bentley. A wonderful compendium of algorithms, puzzles, and creative solutions.

Moving Forward

UNIX And Linux System Administration Handbook, 5th Edition, by Evi Nemeth et al.

Introduction to Algorithms by Thomas Cormen, Charles Leiserson, Ron Rivest, and Clifford Stein. This is the standard serving of vegetables.

Expert C Programming: Deep C Secrets, by Peter van der Linden. C is a jungle of Claymore mines, punji stakes, and unnaturally large insects. Oh my god, we are all going to die. Learn precisely how your demise will come in this disturbing novella. (This book could also go in the Security category, below.)

Higher-Order Perl, by Mark Jason Dominus. A fun and practical (modulo Perl) course on functional programming. Professor Frisby‚Äôs Mostly Adequate Guide To Functional Programming is somewhat along the same lines, using JavaScript.

Effective Modern C++, by Scott Meyers. Helps keep the madness at bay.

Operating Systems

The Design of the Unix Operating System, by Bach; or, The Design and Implementation of the FreeBSD Operating System, by McKusick and Neville-Neil. Reading both might be overkill, but that didn‚Äôt stop me.

Windows Internals, 7th Edition, by Pavel Yosifovich, Mark Russinovich, David Solomon, and Alex Ionescu. A complex but thorough design, well implemented and wonderfully documented.

TCP/IP Illustrated, vol. 1: The Protocols, by Richard Stevens. Solid bedrock for understanding networking.

Security

The Protection Of Information In Computer Systems, by Jerome Saltzer and Michael Schroeder. Foundational.

Cryptography Engineering, by Niels Ferguson, Bruce Schneier, and Tadayoshi Kohno. Practical and accessible.

The Art of Software Security Assessment, by Mark Dowd, John McDonald, and Justin Schuh. A thorough guide to theory and practice.
