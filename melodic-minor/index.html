The Melodic Minor Scale

7 September 2022

The Melodic Minor scale (also Jazz Minor or Ionian ♭3 scale) is just like the Major scale (also Ionian mode) but with a ♭3. In C, it’s C, D, E♭, F, G, A, B, C.

It’s a bit of an odd beast, in a few ways. First, by convention it is used in ascending lines only; when descending, people tend to use the Natural Minor (also Aeolian mode). However, I find it fun to buck convention and use it descending as well. In this article, I’ll use “Melodic Minor” to refer to the ascending form only, whether the melody is going up or down.

Second, it has has the same Vgrunk –  i cadence as Harmonic Minor, but without the 3 half-step leap from the 6th to the 7th degrees (e.g. F♮ to G♯ in A Harmonic Minor). Instead, it has a normal major 6th (F♯ in A Melodic Minor).

Third, it’s just friggin’ odd, if you work it right. It feels really true, to me.

(Note: I notate Melodic Minor as if it were in the Major key (C here) with ♭3, rather than as the Minor key (Cm = E♭) with ♯6 and ♯7. I find it easier to read and write when the number of accidentals is minimal.)

The Chords

Flatting the 3rd of an otherwise major scale has some strange effects on the chords the scale contains. Let’s take a look:

You’ve got the m/maj7 i and augmented III you expect from Harmonic Minor, but also the IV is a dominant 7, and the vi and vii are both half-diminished. To me it adds up to a surprising sound.

There’s also a delightful little weirdie: the suspended 4 chord of the 7th scale degree is a suspended diminished 4. And the 5, F, is diminished, too. In C, that’s B, E♭, F.

A diminished 4th is the same pitch as the major 3rd, of course (D♯ = E♭). So why not notate it as a D♯, and call this a major chord with a ♭5? (B, D♯, F.) You certainly could. Here it is both ways:

Melodic Patterns

On the guitar, it requires some fairly odd fretboard patterns that (for me, at least) are a great knuckle-busting workout. Here, I’ve chosen a pattern that I find sounds like a nice melody, and is good for working on hammer-ons and pull-offs. I follow the same pattern of scale degrees:

④: 1grunk —  2
③: 3grunk —  5
②: 6grunk —  7
①: 8grunk —  9grunk —  10grunk — 8
②: 6grunk —  7grunk —  5
③: 4grunk —  5grunk —  3
④: 1grunk —  2grunk —  7grunk —  1

as I move up through the modes of Melodic Minor. This also takes us up to the 22nd fret, where that legato playing is extra tough. I cannot do this smoothly yet, at all. For maximum mystique, I do as much of it as I can without picking: start each string with a hammer-on from nowhere. Good practice!
