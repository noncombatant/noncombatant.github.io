Review: Tech 21 Fly Rig 5

26 September 2015

I hate guitar amplifiers. They‚Äôre heavy, unwieldy, noisy, highly directional, and too loud. I obviously can‚Äôt play through an amplifier in my apartment. I live in SF, so driving to gigs is a drag because parking; yet there‚Äôs no chance I could take an amp on public transport. To solve this problem, I bought a bunch of pre-amps and speaker emulators to and the winner is the Tech 21 Fly Rig 5. I might review the runners-up in a later post. Here, I‚Äôll talk about the Fly Rig.

The Fly Rig, along with everything else I need, fits in my guitar case. This is now all I need to bring to rehearsal and gigs:

I plug the Fly Rig into our PA for rehearsal, and it sounds great. Katya can control my volume and the overall mix easily, so our volume and EQ don‚Äôt get out of control (as happens so often when guitar players control their amp volume). The Fly Rig has all the basic sounds I need:

  • Sans Amp speaker emulation with 3-band EQ, Level, and Drive controls.
  • A decent spring reverb emulation. I love reverb and almost always leave it dialed to 3:00 or 5:00!
  • Distortion with Level, Tone, and Drive controls.
  • Independent boost with selectable level (Hot).
  • Tap-tempo delay ‚Äî fundamental to my playing style. As a bonus it has a Drift control for chorus-y delay effects. You can get a decent warbly chorus by turning Drift up and Level, Repeats, and Time down.

Each control has an LED light inside, so you can easily see which options are turned on and what their knobs are set at:

When recording at home or in the studio, I tend to use more delays, a looper pedal, and a dedicated reverb and tremolo box (the beautiful and perfect Strymon Flint). But carrying just 1 case to a gig is so convenient that I will give up the extra delays!

Conclusion

If you share my view of amps, definitely get one of these or something else from Tech 21. I love the Fly Rig.

Good:

  • Easy to use: you can just turn every knob to noon and start playing immediately.
  • Reasonable price ($250).
  • Usable as a headphone amp (with a mono-to-stereo adapter).

Bad:

  • For some reason, the Fly Rig powers up with all options turned on. This is loud if your channel on the mixing board is not set to 0 when you turn on. It seems like that could damage something.
  • No stereo output. I love stereo delay, and when you use the Fly Rig as a headphone amp, unless you have a mono-to-stereo adapter, you‚Äôll only get sound in 1 ear.
  • Requires a special AC adapter: 12V, 150mA instead of the more standard Boss-style 9V. The adapter that comes with the Fly Rig is not as robust as the excellent Visual Sound ones. Visual Sound does sell a 12V adapter.

Ugly: Only some of the knobs come with rubber grippers on them. The gripper-less knobs are very hard to use. If you like, you can let Tech 21 nickel-and-dime you: They sell extra grippers, 10 for $7. Plus shipping.

The Fly Rig is almost perfect! I hope never to use a guitar amp ever again.
